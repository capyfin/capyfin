# Moat Framework — 5-Dimension Competitive Advantage Assessment

## Overview

An economic moat is a durable competitive advantage that protects a company's profits from competitors over the long term. Companies with wide moats can sustain above-average returns on capital for extended periods because competitors cannot easily replicate their advantages.

Moats matter because they determine the sustainability of current economics. A company earning 30% operating margins with no moat will see those margins erode as competitors enter. A company earning 20% margins with a wide moat may sustain or expand those margins for a decade.

This framework evaluates moats across 5 dimensions. Each dimension is scored **None**, **Narrow**, or **Wide**. The overall moat rating synthesizes the 5 scores.

---

## Dimension 1: Network Effects

**Definition:** The product or platform becomes more valuable as more users or participants join. Each new user adds value for all existing users, creating a self-reinforcing growth loop.

**Scoring Criteria:**

- **Wide:** The product's core value proposition depends on network size. Removing 50% of users would fundamentally break the product. Examples: Visa/Mastercard payment network, Meta social graph, marketplace platforms (eBay, Airbnb).
- **Narrow:** Network effects exist but are not the primary value driver, or they operate within limited segments. The product would still function at half scale, but with reduced utility. Examples: enterprise collaboration tools, review platforms.
- **None:** The product's value is independent of user count. One user gets the same value whether 100 or 100 million others use it. Examples: most consumer hardware, traditional retail, commodity services.

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. Does each additional user make the product more valuable for existing users?
2. Is there a critical mass threshold below which the product would lose its core value?
3. Are the network effects local (geographic, industry) or global?
4. Can the network effects be bootstrapped by a well-funded competitor?
5. Are there multi-sided network effects (e.g., buyers + sellers + advertisers)?

---

## Dimension 2: Switching Costs

**Definition:** The friction that makes it painful, expensive, or risky for customers to switch to a competitor. High switching costs lock in customers even when alternatives offer comparable or slightly better products.

**Scoring Criteria:**

- **Wide:** Switching would require 6+ months of migration effort, significant financial cost, or substantial business risk. The product is deeply embedded in customer workflows, data, or regulatory compliance. Examples: enterprise ERP systems (SAP, Oracle), core banking platforms, medical device ecosystems.
- **Narrow:** Switching is inconvenient but feasible within weeks to months. Customers may face data migration costs, retraining needs, or short-term productivity loss. Examples: CRM platforms, cloud infrastructure (moderate migration effort), professional software tools.
- **None:** Customers can switch with minimal friction — days or less. No significant data lock-in, contractual barriers, or retraining required. Examples: most consumer apps, commodity services, retail purchases.

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. How long would it take a customer to fully migrate to a competitor?
2. What data, integrations, or customizations would be lost in a switch?
3. Are there contractual lock-in periods (multi-year agreements)?
4. Is there a learning curve associated with the product that users have already invested in?
5. Would switching create business continuity risk for the customer?

---

## Dimension 3: Cost Advantages

**Definition:** The company can produce goods or deliver services at a structurally lower cost than competitors. These advantages are durable — not easily replicated by spending more or trying harder.

**Scoring Criteria:**

- **Wide:** The company has a >15% cost advantage over the next-best competitor, driven by structural factors (massive scale, proprietary process, resource access). Competitors cannot close the gap without fundamentally changing their business model. Examples: Walmart (distribution scale), TSMC (process technology), Saudi Aramco (resource access).
- **Narrow:** The company has a 5-15% cost advantage from moderate scale, geographic positioning, or process efficiency. The advantage is real but potentially closable by a well-capitalized competitor over several years. Examples: regional utilities with favorable geography, mid-tier manufacturers with process optimization.
- **None:** The company operates at similar cost levels to competitors. No structural advantage in production, distribution, or procurement. Examples: most service businesses, companies competing primarily on features or brand.

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. What is the company's cost position relative to its closest competitors?
2. Are the cost advantages driven by scale (volume), process (proprietary technology), or location (geography, resource access)?
3. Can a competitor with sufficient capital replicate the cost advantage within 5 years?
4. Is the cost advantage reflected in consistently higher margins than peers?
5. Does the company pass cost advantages through as lower prices (volume strategy) or retain them as higher margins (premium strategy)?

---

## Dimension 4: Intangible Assets

**Definition:** Non-physical assets that provide competitive advantages — brands, patents, regulatory licenses, proprietary data, or trade secrets that competitors cannot easily replicate.

**Scoring Criteria:**

- **Wide:** The company holds intangible assets that are practically irreplaceable and provide sustained pricing power or market access. Brand commands >15% price premium. Patent portfolio blocks competition for 5+ years. Regulatory license creates a near-monopoly. Examples: luxury brands (Hermès, LVMH), pharmaceutical patents (Eli Lilly, Novo Nordisk), regulated utilities, defense contractors with security clearances.
- **Narrow:** Intangible assets provide some competitive advantage but are partially replicable or have limited duration. Brand has modest pricing power (5-15% premium). Patents expire within 3-5 years. Regulatory barriers can be overcome with effort. Examples: consumer brands with moderate loyalty, technology companies with short-cycle patents, professional services firms with reputation advantages.
- **None:** No meaningful intangible asset advantage. Brand is not a purchase driver. No significant patent protection. Regulatory environment is open to new entrants. Examples: commodity businesses, generic manufacturers, undifferentiated service providers.

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. Does the brand allow the company to charge a price premium over unbranded/generic alternatives?
2. How many years of patent protection remain for key products?
3. Are there regulatory licenses, certifications, or approvals that take years to obtain?
4. Does the company possess proprietary data that improves its product and cannot be replicated by competitors?
5. Would a new entrant with unlimited capital still need 5+ years to replicate these intangible assets?

---

## Dimension 5: Efficient Scale

**Definition:** The market the company serves is naturally limited in size, so it only supports a small number of profitable competitors. New entrants face the unattractive prospect of competing for insufficient demand to cover their fixed costs.

**Scoring Criteria:**

- **Wide:** The market naturally supports only 1-3 profitable competitors. Adding another competitor would make the economics unworkable for everyone. Examples: natural monopolies (pipelines, rail networks), payment processors, aircraft manufacturers (Boeing/Airbus duopoly), stock exchanges.
- **Narrow:** The market supports a limited number of competitors (3-7) and new entry is difficult but not impossible. Existing players have established positions but are not immune to well-resourced challengers. Examples: major cloud providers, wireless carriers, large-cap defense contractors.
- **None:** The market is large enough to support many profitable competitors. Entry barriers from market size are low. Examples: restaurants, retail, most consumer goods, fragmented service industries.

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. How many profitable competitors exist in this market?
2. Would a new entrant's investment in capacity or infrastructure cause returns for all players to fall below cost of capital?
3. Is the market niche enough that it naturally limits the number of viable competitors?
4. Are there high fixed costs that create minimum efficient scale barriers?
5. Has any new entrant successfully entered this market in the last 10 years?

---

## Overall Moat Rating

Synthesize the 5 dimensions into an overall rating:

- **Wide Moat**: At least 2 dimensions rated Wide, OR 1 Wide + 2 Narrow. The company has multiple, reinforcing competitive advantages that should sustain above-average returns for 10+ years.
- **Narrow Moat**: At least 2 dimensions rated Narrow, OR 1 Wide with no supporting Narrow ratings. The company has meaningful competitive advantages but they may erode over 5-10 years without reinforcement.
- **No Moat**: Fewer than 2 Narrow ratings. The company operates in a competitive market without structural advantages. Current margins are at risk from competition.

When the scores are ambiguous (e.g., 1 Narrow and 1 borderline Narrow), lean toward the lower rating and state what evidence would change the call.

## Moat Trend Assessment

The moat trend is as important as the current rating. A narrow moat that is strengthening may be more attractive than a wide moat that is eroding.

**Strengthening indicators:**
- Market share gains for 3+ consecutive years
- Expanding margins while competitors' margins compress
- Increasing switching costs (more integrations, deeper embedding)
- Network effects compounding (user growth accelerating, engagement rising)
- Patent portfolio expanding in core areas

**Stable indicators:**
- Consistent market share over 3+ years
- Margins in line with historical range
- Competitive landscape unchanged
- No major technological disruptions on the horizon

**Eroding indicators:**
- Market share losses for 2+ consecutive years
- Margin compression without clear strategic rationale (investment phase)
- Key patents expiring without replacement pipeline
- Competitors successfully replicating previously unique advantages
- Technological disruption undermining core advantages (e.g., cloud disrupting on-premise lock-in)
- Customer concentration increasing (fewer, more powerful buyers)

State the trend explicitly: **Strengthening**, **Stable**, or **Eroding** — with specific evidence supporting the assessment.
