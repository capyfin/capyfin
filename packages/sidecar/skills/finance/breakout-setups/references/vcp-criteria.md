# VCP Scoring Criteria — Breakout Setup Evaluation Framework

## Overview

The **Volatility Contraction Pattern (VCP)** is a base pattern where price contractions get progressively tighter before a breakout. This framework scores breakout candidates on a 0-100 scale across four dimensions. While named after the VCP, the scoring dimensions apply to all breakout patterns (cup-and-handle, flat base, ascending base) because the underlying principles are universal: Stage 2 trend confirmation, tightening price action, volume confirmation, and relative strength.

**Why VCP scoring works:** Volatility contraction signals that selling pressure is drying up. Each successive tighter contraction means fewer sellers remain. When volume drops to its lowest point near the pivot, it indicates the available supply has been absorbed by institutional buyers. A breakout from this point of minimal supply, accompanied by a volume surge, has the highest probability of sustaining an advance.

The scoring system quantifies these signals into a single 0-100 score that allows comparison across setups and pattern types.

---

## Dimension 1: Stage 2 Uptrend Confirmation (25 points)

**Why it matters:** Breakouts have the highest success rate when the stock is already in a Stage 2 uptrend — price above rising moving averages. Stage 2 means institutions are accumulating. Breakouts from Stage 1 (basing) or Stage 3 (topping) have much lower success rates.

### Scoring Criteria

**Price above rising 200-day MA (10 points):**
- The 200-day moving average must be rising (positive slope for at least 1 month)
- Price must be above the 200-day MA
- If the 200-day MA is flat or declining, this criterion is NOT met even if price is above it
- Full 10 points if both conditions are met; 0 points if either fails

**Price above rising 50-day MA or 10-week MA (10 points):**
- The 50-day MA (or 10-week MA, which is approximately equivalent) must be rising
- Price must be above the 50-day MA, OR within 3% of it during a constructive pullback to the line
- A pullback to the 50-day MA that holds on low volume is constructive and still qualifies
- A close below the 50-day MA on heavy volume disqualifies; 0 points
- Full 10 points if the condition is met; 0 points if it fails

**200-day MA slope positive for 1+ month (5 points):**
- The 200-day MA must have been rising for at least 20 trading days (approximately 1 calendar month)
- This confirms the long-term trend is established, not just a recent cross above the MA
- Full 5 points if met; 0 points if the MA just started rising or is flat

### Score Ranges
- **25/25**: All three criteria met — strong Stage 2 uptrend confirmed
- **15-20/25**: Two of three criteria met — likely Stage 2 but with a weakness (e.g., 50-day MA pullback)
- **5-10/25**: Only one criterion met — questionable Stage 2, may be early Stage 1 or late Stage 3
- **0/25**: No criteria met — stock is NOT in Stage 2. Breakout setups in non-Stage 2 environments should score very low overall

## Dimension 2: Contraction Tightness (25 points)

**Why it matters:** The hallmark of institutional accumulation is progressively tighter contractions. Each tighter pullback means fewer weak holders remain. The tightest contraction near the pivot point signals that the selling is exhausted — the stock is coiled and ready to move.

### Contraction Measurement

A **contraction** is measured as the percentage decline from a local high to the subsequent local low within the base pattern:
- **T1 (first contraction):** The initial pullback from the high. Typically the deepest. Ideal range: 25-35%.
- **T2 (second contraction):** The next pullback after a partial recovery. Should be tighter than T1. Ideal range: 15-25%.
- **T3 (third contraction):** The final contraction before the pivot. Should be the tightest. Ideal range: 8-15%.
- **T4+ (additional contractions):** Even tighter. Some of the best setups show 4 contractions with the last being <8%.

### Scoring Criteria

**Each successive contraction tighter than the prior (10 points):**
- Compare T1 > T2 > T3 in depth percentage
- All contractions must show clear tightening — each one shallower than the last
- If T2 is deeper than T1, or T3 is deeper than T2, the tightening is NOT confirmed; 0 points
- Full 10 points if every contraction is demonstrably tighter than the prior one

**3+ contractions with clear tightening (10 points):**
- Minimum 2 contractions required for any scoring (if only 1 contraction exists, score 0 on this sub-criterion)
- 2 contractions with tightening = 5 points
- 3+ contractions with tightening = full 10 points
- 4+ contractions with progressive tightening = full 10 points (bonus confidence, not bonus points)

**Deepest contraction ≤35% (5 points):**
- The first (deepest) contraction should not exceed 35% — this indicates excessive selling pressure
- T1 ≤ 25%: 5 points (tight)
- T1 25-35%: 5 points (acceptable)
- T1 > 35%: 0 points (too deep — this is a correction, not a contraction)
- Exception: During severe market corrections, T1 up to 40% may be acceptable if subsequent contractions show strong tightening

### Score Ranges
- **25/25**: 3+ contractions with perfect tightening progression and T1 ≤ 35%
- **15-20/25**: 2 contractions with tightening, or 3+ contractions with T1 slightly > 35%
- **5-10/25**: 2 contractions but tightening is marginal, or only 1 clear contraction
- **0/25**: No identifiable contractions, or contractions are widening (getting deeper — opposite of VCP)

## Dimension 3: Volume Dry-Up (25 points)

**Why it matters:** Volume is the single most important confirmation signal in technical analysis. Declining volume during a base means sellers are exhausted. The lowest volume near the pivot means there is minimal supply to overcome. A breakout from a volume dry-up point, accompanied by a volume surge, is the most reliable setup in technical analysis.

### Volume Analysis Method

Compare volume at different stages of the base:

1. **Volume during the prior advance** — This is the baseline. The advance should have occurred on above-average volume.
2. **Volume during the left side of the base** — The initial correction. Volume is often heavy here as weak holders sell.
3. **Volume on the right side of the base** — Should be declining. This is where the dry-up occurs.
4. **Volume near the pivot point** — Should be at or near the lowest point in the entire base.

### Scoring Criteria

**Volume declining on right side of base (10 points):**
- Compare average volume in the last 2 weeks of the base to the first 2 weeks
- Volume in the last 2 weeks should be at least 20% lower than the first 2 weeks
- A clear visual decline in volume bars from left to right confirms the dry-up
- Full 10 points if the decline is evident; 0 points if volume is flat or increasing

**Below-average volume during consolidation (10 points):**
- Compare recent volume (last 2 weeks) to the 50-day average volume
- Volume should be below the 50-day average — ideally 50-70% of average
- Full 10 points if recent volume is <80% of 50-day average
- 5 points if recent volume is 80-100% of 50-day average
- 0 points if recent volume is above the 50-day average (no dry-up)

**Volume at multi-week low near pivot point (5 points):**
- The day or week with the lowest volume should be near the pivot point (within the last contraction)
- This is the "dry-up point" — the moment of minimum supply
- Full 5 points if the lowest volume day/week in the base is within the final contraction
- 0 points if the lowest volume was earlier in the base (not near the pivot)

### Score Ranges
- **25/25**: Clear volume dry-up with lowest volume near the pivot — textbook setup
- **15-20/25**: Volume declining on the right side but not dramatically, or lowest point slightly away from pivot
- **5-10/25**: Some volume decline but not convincing, or volume is around average
- **0/25**: Volume is increasing or flat through the base — no dry-up, setup is suspect

## Dimension 4: Relative Strength (RS) Line (25 points)

**Why it matters:** The RS line compares a stock's performance to the S&P 500 (or relevant benchmark). A rising RS line means the stock is outperforming the market. The best breakout setups occur when the RS line is at or near a new high — this means institutions are actively choosing this stock over the market.

An RS line at a new high while the stock's price is still in a base is one of the most bullish signals in technical analysis. It means the stock is outperforming even while consolidating — institutions are accumulating aggressively.

### RS Line Calculation

RS Line = Stock Price / S&P 500 Price (or sector ETF price for sector-relative analysis)

- Plot over time to see the trend
- Compare to the 52-week high of the RS line
- A rising RS line on a daily/weekly chart means outperformance

### Scoring Criteria

**RS line at or near 52-week high (15 points):**
- **At new 52-week high**: 15 points — strongest possible signal
- **Within 5% of 52-week high**: 12 points — near-high, very strong
- **Within 10% of 52-week high**: 8 points — showing relative strength
- **More than 10% below 52-week high**: 0 points — underperforming, not a leader

**RS line trending up, not flat or declining (10 points):**
- The RS line should show a clear upward trend over the last 3 months
- Full 10 points if the RS line is in a clear uptrend
- 5 points if the RS line is flat (not declining, but not leading)
- 0 points if the RS line is declining — the stock is underperforming the market

### Score Ranges
- **25/25**: RS line at new high and trending up — this stock is a market leader
- **15-20/25**: RS line near high (within 5-10%) and trending up — strong relative performance
- **5-10/25**: RS line trending up but well below its high — moderate relative strength
- **0/25**: RS line declining — the stock is underperforming the market. Do NOT recommend breakout setups with declining RS.

## Score Interpretation and Action Guidance

### 90-100: Textbook Setup
- **All four dimensions strongly met**
- This is the highest-probability breakout setup
- Action: Ready for breakout. Define pivot, set a volume threshold (1.5x average), and watch for the breakout day.
- Risk management: Invalidation at the low of the last contraction. Risk typically 5-8% from pivot.

### 80-89: Strong Setup
- **One minor weakness** but three dimensions are strong
- Still a high-quality setup worthy of attention
- Action: Ready for breakout, but note the weakness. If the weakness is in volume (no clear dry-up), wait for volume confirmation on breakout day.
- Risk management: Same as textbook, but may want slightly smaller position size due to the minor weakness.

### 70-79: Good Setup — Developing
- **Clear positive traits** but the pattern needs more time or has one notable deficiency
- Action: Add to watch list. Monitor for improvement in the weak dimension. The pattern may mature into an 80+ setup with another 1-2 weeks of consolidation.
- Risk management: Not yet actionable. Wait for the score to improve before committing capital.

### 60-69: Developing — Watch Only
- **Needs more time** or has a notable weakness that reduces reliability
- Action: Watch list only. The pattern may develop into a valid setup, or it may deteriorate. Check back in 1-2 weeks.
- Risk management: Do NOT act on developing setups. The failure rate is too high.

### Below 60: Not Ready
- **Missing key criteria** — this is not a valid breakout setup at this time
- Action: Remove from consideration. Either the trend is not Stage 2, the pattern is not constructive, volume is wrong, or RS is declining.
- Note: A stock scoring below 60 may still be a good company — the scoring measures setup quality, not business quality.

## Common Pitfalls

These mistakes lead to failed breakout trades. Each is a pattern-quality red flag:

1. **Late-Stage Bases (4th+ base in an advance):** Each successive base in an uptrend has a lower success rate. The first and second bases are the most reliable. By the 4th base, the stock may be entering Stage 3 (topping). Count the bases since the start of the uptrend.

2. **Wide-and-Loose Patterns:** Bases with wild price swings (>40% depth) or wide weekly ranges are not constructive. They indicate institutional selling, not accumulation. A valid base should progressively tighten, not chop.

3. **Declining RS Line:** Never buy a breakout when the RS line is declining. Even if the pattern looks perfect, a declining RS line means institutions are rotating OUT of this stock. You're fighting the flow.

4. **Ignoring Volume on Breakout:** A breakout that occurs on average or below-average volume is suspect. Demand volume surge of at least 1.5x average on the breakout day. Low-volume breakouts frequently fail.

5. **Overhead Supply Ignorance:** If the stock is breaking out from a base that formed after a 50%+ decline, there is massive overhead supply — previous holders looking to sell on any rally. These breakouts have lower success rates. Best setups form after corrections of 15-35%.

6. **Market Regime Ignorance:** Even textbook setups fail in market downtrends. Always check the market regime before acting on individual setups. During Downtrend environments, 80%+ of breakouts fail.

7. **Premature Entry:** Buying before the pivot is confirmed (trying to "get in early") negates the entire setup. The pivot exists for a reason — it's the confirmation point. Wait for it.
